What is Electric Charge?

Electric charge is a fundamental property of matter, just like mass. It's what makes particles attract or repel each other through electromagnetic forces.

Elementary Charge
e = 1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C
This is the smallest amount of charge that can exist freely. One electron carries -e, and one proton carries +e.

Atomic Structure: Protons (+) in nucleus, Electrons (-) in orbitals

Types of Electric Charge

➕
Positive Charge
Carried by protons. When an object loses electrons, it becomes positively charged. Symbol: +q or +e
➖
Negative Charge
Carried by electrons. When an object gains electrons, it becomes negatively charged. Symbol: -q or -e
⚬
Neutral Charge
Equal numbers of protons and electrons. No net charge. Symbol: 0 or neutral

Coulomb's Law
F = k(q₁q₂)/r²
The force between two charges depends on:
• The amount of each charge (q₁, q₂)
• The distance between them (r) - force gets weaker as distance increases
• k = 8.99 × 10⁹ N⋅m²/C² (Coulomb's constant)

Where Do We Find Electric Charge?

⚛️
Inside Atoms
Every atom contains protons (+) in the nucleus and electrons (-) in orbitals around it.
⚡
Lightning
Static electricity builds up in clouds until it discharges as lightning bolts.
🧲
Static Electricity
Rubbing materials together transfers electrons, creating static charge.
🔋
Batteries
Chemical reactions separate charges to create electrical energy.
Electric Circuits
Moving electrons through wires create electric current to power devices.
🧬
Living Things
Nerve signals and muscle contractions use electrical charges.

Conservation of Electric Charge

Electric charge cannot be created or destroyed - it can only be transferred from one object to another. This is called the Law of Conservation of Charge.

Key Principle:

The total amount of positive and negative charge in the universe always stays the same. When we "create" charge, we're actually separating existing positive and negative charges.
